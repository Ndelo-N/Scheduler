# Working Around Your Studies: How This System Works for You

You are a student first. That single idea is the reason this system exists.

When you take on work as a student assistant, you are holding two timetables at once — the one your lecturers set, and the one your work expects. Most scheduling tools only understand the second. They treat you as an employee who happens to study, slotting you into shifts and leaving you to fight for the time your degree needs. This system was built the other way round: it treats you as a student who happens to work, and it shapes your shifts around the life you are already living.

## It knows when you cannot work

Your availability is not an afterthought here. The hours you have told the system you are free are the hours it draws from — not a starting point it bargains away. The recurring gaps in your week for lectures and study stay protected.

The same is true of the moments that matter most. When you have a test, the system keeps that time clear. When exams arrive — in June and again in November — it goes further: it will not place you on a shift the day before you sit a paper, and on the day itself it leaves you a clear hour after your exam ends before any shift can begin. You should be revising or resting, not watching a roster. The system carries that worry so you do not have to.

## It shares the work fairly

Every student assistant has a number of hours they have contracted to work. The system's job is to honour that number for everyone at once — to spread shifts so that no one is quietly carrying too much and no one is left short.

It does not judge you week by week, because a semester does not work that way. Some weeks are heavy with assignments; some are quiet. What matters is where you land over a whole contract period, and the system leaves real room for the natural rise and fall along the way. The aim is simple: that by the time a period closes, you have worked close to what you agreed — without a scramble at the end to make up a shortfall, and without the surprise of discovering you have somehow done too much.

## It keeps an honest count

This is where scheduled hours and worked hours meet. The system does not assume that the shift it planned is the shift that happened — it listens to the clock. Each week, the record of when you signed on and off is read in and compared against what you were rostered for.

That honesty is there to protect you. Your balance — the running tally of where you stand against your contracted hours — is built from the time you worked, not from an estimate. There is a clear, consistent way the edges are handled: arriving a few minutes late, or leaving a little early, is counted the same way for everyone, every time, so there is no guesswork and nothing to argue about. And if something looks unusual — a shift that ran far longer than planned, or one that does not line up with the roster — the system sets it aside for a person to look at rather than silently swallowing it. Where a real exception needs to stand, someone with the authority to do so can record it.

The point of all of this is that you are never in the dark. You can watch your hours adding up toward what you have agreed to, and you can trust that the number in front of you is real.

## When life happens

Plans change. Someone falls ill, a deadline shifts, a family obligation appears. The system expects this. Shifts can be handed over, and it keeps fair track of who covered for whom — so that coverage holds for everyone, and so that no single person ends up unfairly out of pocket on their time. Flexibility and fairness are not in tension here; the system is built so that helping each other out does not quietly create a new imbalance down the line.

## Why it is built this way

There is a thread running through all of this, and it is worth naming. None of us works in isolation. The roster only holds together because each person's hours fit alongside everyone else's. A shift you cannot make is a gap someone else feels; a balance that drifts too far touches the whole team's cover. So the system is always doing two things at once — looking after you as an individual, with your studies, your contracted hours, and your fair share, and looking after the group you are part of.

What you get from that is more than a timetable. It is a little more room to be a student: confidence that your exams are respected, that your hours are counted truthfully, that you will reach what you agreed to without a last-minute crisis, and that when life gets in the way there is a fair path through it. The work is meant to support your studies, not compete with them — and this system exists to keep it that way.
